You know the moment. You're in a lift — an elevator, you have to catch yourself — somewhere in Manhattan or Melbourne, and someone makes a dry comment about nothing in particular, and everyone in the lift sort of smirks but doesn't laugh outright because you don't actually laugh at something in a lift, and you think: *that's it. That's the thing.*

It's not Tayto. It's not Barry's tea. It's not even the summer, which was never that good to begin with. It's the people.

A piece in the Irish Times Abroad section this month gathered reflections from Irish people living overseas on what they genuinely miss about home. The answers were warm, funny, and fairly universal. And chief among them was something that is genuinely hard to export: the particular Irish genius for not taking yourself too seriously.

## The Craic Is Not a Tourist Slogan

It can be tempting, from the outside, to dismiss the idea of the craic as marketing material — shamrocks and leprechauns repackaged for consumption. But the Irish people interviewed for the piece were pointing at something real. It is a specific social texture. The way that self-importance is quietly, mercilessly deflated in Irish social situations. The way that a gathering of people who've never met before can achieve a kind of ease within minutes. The way that someone having a terrible time will still, eventually, describe it as "gas altogether."

In many places in the world — and those of you in New York, London, or Sydney know exactly what we mean — the social register is different. People talk about their work as though it is their identity. Status is worn on the surface. Introductions come with CVs attached. It is not a criticism; it is just different. And for Irish emigrants, the difference can feel, on certain days, like a low-grade homesickness that is very hard to explain to a non-Irish colleague over lunch.

## The Specific Things

The piece also touched on the more tangible, the delightfully specific. The Sunday morning walk before a roast. Watching your county in Croke Park in August. The way an Irish pub — not a plastic Irish pub abroad, a real one, the kind with mismatched chairs and a handwritten GAA fixture on the wall — just smells right. The particular quality of light on a mild day in June, rare as hen's teeth and all the more precious for it.

Food is never far from these conversations. Tayto crisps. A proper sausage sandwich on a Saturday morning. A 99 from an ice cream van that has no business being out on a day that cold. The chips from your chipper in your town — not A chipper, YOUR chipper, the one your parents went to.

Then there are the people who aren't specific at all — who just miss home as a feeling, the particular ease of being among people who know the references, don't need things explained, and assume a shared understanding of the world that took thirty years to build and can't be replicated in any city in the world, however good the Irish community there is.

## Does It Change by Where You Live?

The piece in the Irish Times cast a fairly wide net, but we've noticed over the years that what people miss about home differs a little depending on where they've ended up.

In **Chicago and Boston**, where the Irish community is large and established, the miss tends to be more specific — the county, the parish, the way your mother makes tea. The community abroad is strong enough that many of the social textures of Irish life are replicated, but something always remains out of reach.

In **London**, proximity to home makes the absence more pointed. You can be on a plane in two hours. You could go back any time. The fact that you don't — for whatever complicated reason — gives the missing a particular edge.

In **Sydney and Melbourne**, the distance is physical and temporal as well as emotional. The time difference means big moments at home happen while you're asleep. The seasons are inverted. Christmas is in the heat. And the craic, when you find it in an Irish pub in Bondi or Fitzroy, is all the more precious for being so far from where it started.

## Tell Us What You Miss

We want to know. Not the polite answer, not the one that will go on a tea towel — the real one. The thing that catches you by surprise on a Tuesday afternoon and makes you momentarily ache for somewhere that is still, on some level, home.
