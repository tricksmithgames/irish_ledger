If you've been keeping a vague eye on the Irish property market from Boston or Brisbane, thinking that surely things have settled down since the worst of the post-crash years, we have some difficult news.

The number of new homes beginning construction in Ireland collapsed by more than 75% in 2025. Just over 16,000 housing commencements were recorded last year, compared to more than 69,300 in 2024. Economists at the Banking and Payments Federation of Ireland have described the situation as a "steep decline" that poses serious risks to future supply — meaning the shortage of homes will almost certainly get worse before it gets better.

For anyone considering a move back to Ireland in the next two or three years, this is essential reading.

## What Happened?

The dramatic 2024 figure wasn't a sign of a housing recovery — it was a bubble of activity caused by developers rushing to begin construction before government development-levy waivers expired. When those waivers ended, so did the incentive to break ground quickly. The result is an artificial cliff: the 2024 numbers were inflated, and 2025 paid the price.

The declines were particularly severe in Dublin's suburbs. Commencements fell by nearly 84% in Fingal and South Dublin — precisely the areas where families with young children might hope to find an affordable three-bedroom house with a bit of a garden. The very supply that returning emigrants tend to need most has effectively evaporated.

There is a partial silver lining: housing completions actually rose in 2025, reaching 36,284 — the highest figure since 2009 — as projects begun in earlier years were finished. So the backlog from the 2024 rush is still feeding through. But a commencement today becomes a completion in two to three years. The pipeline beyond 2026 and 2027 is thin.

## What Does This Mean for Renters?

The rental market is where the immediate pain is felt, and it has not eased. The average monthly rent for a two-bedroom apartment nationally reached €2,086 in the final quarter of 2025. Nationwide rental stock fell 22% in the year to February 2026 — fewer properties available, at higher prices.

To put that in context: a two-bed apartment in Dublin currently costs a couple renting together around €25,000 per year before bills, food, or childcare. For anyone returning from the US or Australia where wages may be higher but so is purchasing power parity, the sticker shock on Irish rents remains genuinely brutal.

## New Rental Laws: Some Light in the Tunnel

The government enacted new rental legislation from 1 March 2026, and while it does not fix the supply crisis, it does offer meaningful protections for those who do find a place to rent. The key changes:

**Six-year minimum tenancies.** All new tenancies created from 1 March 2026 last for a minimum of six years, renewing automatically in six-year cycles. This is significant protection against the kind of no-fault evictions that left many renters suddenly homeless in previous years.

**A national rent cap.** Rents can now only increase once per year, and by a maximum of 2% — or by inflation (CPI) if that is lower. This applies nationally, replacing the old Rent Pressure Zone system.

For anyone entering the rental market in Ireland for the first time, these laws offer considerably more security than existed even a year ago. The problem remains finding a place to rent in the first place.

## If You're Thinking of Buying

The picture for buyers is challenging. Property prices rose 7% in the year to December 2025, following an 8.9% increase the year before. Irish homes are now selling for an average of 52% more than their pre-Covid levels, according to Daft.ie data from January 2026.

The fundamental problem — more people wanting homes than homes available — will not improve while commencements remain depressed. If the 2026 construction figures do not show a significant rebound, the shortage will compound into the late 2020s. Economists suggest Ireland needs 60,000 to 70,000 new homes per year to meet demand; current delivery is roughly half that.

## What You Can Do From Abroad

If you are seriously considering a move home in the next 12 to 36 months, here is practical advice:

**Start watching the market now.** Daft.ie and MyHome.ie list properties in real time and give you a realistic sense of what is available in specific counties and towns.

**Consider rural and regional.** Prices and availability are considerably better outside the main cities. Remote work has made Cork city, Galway, Limerick, Kilkenny, and smaller towns genuinely viable options for many knowledge-economy workers.

**Talk to a mortgage broker early.** Irish mortgage rules require a deposit of 10–20% of the purchase price, and rules around how foreign income is assessed vary by lender. Starting that conversation early is advisable.

**Watch for government schemes.** The Help to Buy scheme and First Home shared equity scheme exist to assist first-time buyers. Check revenue.ie and firsthomescheme.ie for current eligibility.

The housing crisis in Ireland is a political failure sustained over many years. It is not something that will resolve itself quickly. But it is not uniform either — and with research, planning, and a good dose of realism, moving home remains possible for many diaspora families. Just don't expect it to be easy.
