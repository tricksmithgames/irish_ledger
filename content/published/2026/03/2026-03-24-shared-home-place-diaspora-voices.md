The Irish government has a message for the millions of Irish people living abroad: we want to know who you are, what you carry with you, and what Ireland still means to you.

Minister for Foreign Affairs Helen McEntee chose an appropriately symbolic setting — the John F. Kennedy Presidential Library and Museum in Boston — to launch "Shared Home Place: Diaspora Voices" during St Patrick's week. It is a government-backed project inviting Irish communities around the world to come together and explore their shared heritage, cultural identity, and their ongoing relationship with the place they came from.

For readers in the United States, the United Kingdom, Australia, Canada, and beyond, this is an initiative worth knowing about — because it's asking for your voice.

## What Is "Shared Home Place: Diaspora Voices"?

At its heart, the initiative is a listening project. Supported by the Shared Island Fund, it is designed to bring Irish communities abroad together — across all traditions and backgrounds, north and south — to explore their connections to Ireland's past, present, and future.

The programme will be rolled out across 2026 on a pilot basis, focused initially on regions with large, established Irish populations — places like Boston, Chicago, New York, London, Birmingham, Sydney, and Melbourne. It will be led by Irish embassies and consulates, but is intended to be rooted in local communities rather than driven from Dublin.

Events could take a range of forms: town halls, fireside conversations, panel discussions, citizen assembly-style fora, academic research projects, or even online engagements for communities that are more dispersed. The emphasis is on participation and storytelling, not lectures or presentations.

## Why This Matters for the Diaspora

For Irish communities abroad, the initiative offers something genuinely valuable: official recognition that the diaspora's experience is complex, layered, and worth exploring seriously.

Too often, engagement with the Irish abroad takes the shape of St Patrick's Day parades and tourist board promotions. "Shared Home Place" is a different kind of outreach. It asks what your relationship with Ireland actually looks like in 2026 — for someone whose great-grandparents left Connaught during the Famine, but also for someone who left Cork in 2012 and is still trying to figure out whether they'll ever go back.

It is cross-community in design, which means it is as much for Irish people of unionist background in Britain as it is for third-generation Irish-Americans. The Shared Island Fund, which backs it, was created precisely to explore the identities and connections that exist across the traditions of the island, regardless of political affiliation.

## Mark Your Calendar: The Fourth Global Irish Civic Forum

One event worth watching is the Fourth Global Irish Civic Forum, scheduled for April 2026. This gathering brings together representatives of Irish communities from around the world and provides an opportunity to engage directly with the Irish government on issues affecting the diaspora. Attendance or participation in advocacy ahead of it could be a way for Irish Ledger readers to make their voices heard at a policy level.

## The Bigger Picture: Ireland's Diaspora Strategy 2026–2031

The "Shared Home Place" project is part of a broader government effort — the Diaspora Strategy 2026–2031 — which is the Irish government's attempt to build a more coherent and sustained relationship with the 70 million or so people worldwide who claim Irish heritage or connection.

We'll be covering the Strategy in more detail in the coming weeks, but for now, the key thing to know is that the government is, at least rhetorically, moving away from treating the diaspora as a once-a-year St Patrick's Day photo opportunity and toward something that takes our communities seriously year-round.

Whether that ambition translates into action remains to be seen. We'll be watching, and reporting.

## How to Get Involved

Watch for announcements from your local Irish embassy or consulate about upcoming "Shared Home Place" events in your city. You can check embassy websites or follow their social media channels for scheduling information as the pilot rolls out through 2026.

If you're in Boston, Chicago, New York, London, Sydney, or Melbourne, events are likely to begin in the coming months. If you're in a smaller diaspora community, online participation options are being built into the programme.

In the meantime, we'd invite you to share your own story with us here at Irish Ledger. What does home mean to you? What do you carry from Ireland wherever you've settled? Tell us in the comments or reach out via our social media — **#SharedHomePlaceIL**.
