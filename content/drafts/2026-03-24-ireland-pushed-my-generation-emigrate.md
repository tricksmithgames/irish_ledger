Somewhere in the back of your head, if you're Irish and you left, there's a sentence you've probably heard so many times you stopped questioning it. *Sure there's nothing there for you. Sure the place is too small. Sure you're better off getting out and seeing the world.*

It was said with love. It was said by people who meant well. And it was, in a quiet and devastating way, a lie — or at least a choice disguised as an inevitability.

A piece published in the Irish Times today puts words to something many of us have felt but never fully articulated: that Irish emigration was not just an economic reality but a cultural posture, a learned acceptance that leaving was normal, even necessary. That staying was somehow greedy. That a small island couldn't accommodate all of us.

## The Ghost of Brian Lenihan

The reference point is a famous comment attributed to former Tánaiste Brian Lenihan, who in the wake of the financial crisis framed emigration as almost natural — the unavoidable result of Ireland's limited geography and carrying capacity. The island was too small for us all. Go forth and multiply elsewhere.

It was a framing that suited a government unwilling or unable to do the hard structural work of keeping its people. And it was, perhaps most damagingly, a framing that many Irish people internalised. A generation grew up believing that ambition meant departure. That the smarter, the braver, the more talented you were, the sooner you'd be on a plane out of Dublin Airport.

The Irish Times piece, written by someone who recently visited EPIC — the Irish Emigration Museum in the Docklands — reflects on how the museum's beautiful, celebratory account of Irish diaspora culture sits awkwardly with the reality that many people left not out of adventure but out of desperation, because the country they loved couldn't offer them a home.

## Was This Your Story Too?

For readers in Boston, London, Sydney, or Toronto, the question is likely not abstract. You may have left in the years after the crash, or during, or in the quieter exodus that followed when the headlines moved on but the housing market didn't. You may have had a perfectly good job but simply couldn't see how you'd ever afford to live the life your parents had — a house, stability, roots.

The piece asks: what does it mean that the normalisation of emigration allowed successive Irish governments to avoid the hard choices? Every departure was a pressure valve, a person who wouldn't clog up a hospital waiting list, wouldn't need social housing, wouldn't vote in frustration at election time. Emigration was, for the State, convenient.

That's a thought worth sitting with.

## The Crisis Continues

The uncomfortable truth is that the conditions which shaped that generation's departure haven't changed enough. Housing commencements in Ireland fell by more than 75% last year — from over 69,000 in 2024 to just over 16,000 in 2025. The average rent for a two-bed apartment nationally reached €2,086 per month in the final quarter of 2025. Rental stock is down 22% in the year to February 2026.

New rental laws that came into effect on 1 March — including six-year minimum tenancies and a national rent cap linked to CPI — offer some hope for those currently renting. But for anyone hoping to buy, the pipeline of new homes is emptying out. The same conversation we had in 2012 is happening again in 2026, with different faces and the same structural failures.

## The Right to Stay

What the Irish Times piece ultimately argues — and what we think is worth amplifying — is not that emigration is inherently bad, or that the diaspora is a wound rather than a strength. Of course it is both; it has always been both. The diaspora is extraordinary. The culture Irish people carry around the world is real and alive and worth celebrating.

But the choice to leave should be exactly that: a choice. Not a foregone conclusion. Not a gentle push from a State that found it easier to export its young than to house them.

Staying in Ireland should be a right. It should be something the country is organised around preserving. And judging by the numbers, we're still a long way from that.
